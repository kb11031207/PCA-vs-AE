## About

This capstone studies a fundamental question in unsupervised learning: **when is a nonlinear method (autoencoder) worth the extra cost over a classical linear method (PCA)?** The answer depends on the structure of the data being compressed.

The project pairs a mathematical derivation of both methods including a proof of the known equivalence between PCA and a linear autoencoder  with a computational comparison on two deliberately contrasting datasets:

- **MNIST** handwritten digits, where the data lies on a curved nonlinear manifold and the autoencoder's flexibility pays off.
- **Indian Pines** hyperspectral imagery, where neighboring spectral bands are highly correlated and the underlying structure is approximately linear.

This repository contains the paper itself (PDF) alongside two self-contained Jupyter notebooks that produce every figure and numerical result cited in the paper. Figures are saved as 300 DPI PDF + PNG for direct LaTeX inclusion, and the entire pipeline is reproducible from a fresh Python environment.

## Findings

| Dataset | Reconstruction | 2D Visualization | Classification |
|---|---|---|---|
| MNIST | Autoencoder wins (~36% lower MSE at d=20) | Autoencoder wins | not tested |
| Indian Pines | not tested | PCA wins | PCA wins (≈3 pp higher accuracy, ~2000× faster) |

The autoencoder outperforms PCA on image data, where the underlying manifold is nonlinear. PCA matches or outperforms the autoencoder on hyperspectral data, where the intrinsic structure is approximately linear at a fraction of the compute cost.

A separate sanity check trains a *linear* autoencoder on MNIST and verifies that its reconstruction MSE matches PCA's to within 10⁻⁴, confirming the known theoretical equivalence and isolating the contribution of nonlinearity in the ReLU autoencoder.

## Running the notebooks

Run cells top-to-bottom. Both notebooks are self-contained:

- **`01_mnist_pca_vs_ae.ipynb`** — Loads MNIST via `sklearn.datasets.fetch_openml` (cached after first run). End-to-end runtime ~5 min on CPU (no GPU required).
- **`02_indian_pines_pca_vs_ae.ipynb`** — Downloads the Indian Pines `.mat` files from the EHU mirror into `data/` on first run, then caches. End-to-end runtime ~3 min on CPU.

## Datasets

- **MNIST** — 28×28 grayscale handwritten digits, 10 classes. We use a stratified 10,000-image subsample from the 70k full set.
- **Indian Pines** — 145×145 hyperspectral image, 200 spectral bands, 16 land-cover classes. After dropping unlabeled background pixels: 10,249 labeled samples.

## Reproducibility

All randomness is seeded via `keras.utils.set_random_seed(42)`. Results are deterministic to within the small non-determinism of CPU TensorFlow. Numerical values reported in the paper were taken from a single representative run.

## Dependencies

`numpy`, `pandas`, `matplotlib`, `scikit-learn`, `tensorflow`, `spectral`, `jupyter`, `ipykernel`. Exact versions used during development are in `requirements.txt`.
